Dr. Ryan C. Challener

I am a postdoctoral researcher at Cornell University, where I work on atmospheric characterization of exoplanetary atmospheres, with a particular focus in understanding their two- and three-dimensional properties. I received a B. S. in Physics & Astronomy and a B. A. in Mathematics from the University of Rochester, and earned my Ph. D. in Physics (Planetary Science) from the University of Central Florida.

Publications

The Mid-infrared Map of HD 189733b

This work, led by my graduate student Maura Lally, reported mid-infrared temperature map of the benchmark hot Jupiter HD 189733b. We showed that, for planets with longer orbital periods, linear instrumental trends can mimic planetary rotation signals. However, when combining our JWST data with a Spitzer partial phase curve, we were able to accurately recover the planet signal and measure a precise eastward hotspot offset. ADS

Latitudinal Asymmetry in an Exoplanet Atmosphere

In this work, we presented maps of the canonical hot Jupiter WASP-43b based on a phase-curve (full orbit) observation with JWST NIRSpec using the G395H grism. We showed that the planet has a distinct latitudinal offset in the hottest part of the planet, the first confident detection of latitudinal asymmetry in an explanet atmosphere. Such asymmetries could be due to a number of effects, perhaps most promisingly due to interactions between the planet's magnetic field and its atmosphere. ADS

Leave-one-out Cross Validation

We demonstrated how a statistical technique called leave-one-out cross validation (LOO-CV) can be used to understand the relationship between planetary map models and our observations, helping us to understand the spatial patterns we can measure and providing guidelines to investigating degeneracies, like that seen for WASP-18b (see the map of the planet below). ADS

The Eclipse Mapping Null Space

In this work, we showed that there are mathematical limitations to eclipse mapping, such that some planetary brightness patterns are undetectable. This has two consequences: 1) when comparing theoretical predictions to observed maps, we must transform the predictions to their observable components, and 2) uncertainties on observed maps can be underestimated if not accounting for the null space. ADS

The Broadband Thermal Map of WASP-18b

With the JWST Transiting Exoplanet Community Early Release Science team, I created the first 2D map of an exoplanet using JWST data. WASP-18b has two potential mapping solutions: a "traditional" hotspot or a dayside plateau. Both solutions indicate the presence of strong atmospheric drag, potentially due to a magnetic field interacting the highly-ionized atmosphere. We also detected the presence of water at very high confidence and were able to rule out a previously-proposed high carbon-to-oxygen ratio atmosphere. ADS

Three-dimensional Eclipse Mapping

I developed the Three-dimensional Exoplanet Retrieval from Eclipse spectroscopy of Atmospheres code (ThERESA), which effectively combines two-dimensional eclipse mapping with one-dimensional atmospheric retrieval to infer 3D properties of exoplanet atmospheres. I showed that JWST data will enable 3D characterization of hot Jupiters, with application to synthetic JWST data. ADS

Search for Transits of Proxima Centauri b

In 2016 and 2017, I and collaborators planned Spitzer observations to look for transits of the nearest exoplanet to Earth -- Proxima Centauri b. Unfortunately, we conclusively determined that the planet does not transit, but we learned much about the telescope that can improve future analyses. ADS

Elliptical Aperture Photometry

In this work, I presented a new elliptical-aperture photometry image analysis method to correct for the effects of telescope vibrations on light-curve observations. Vibrations smear the target star on the detector, spilling light from traditional circular apertures, creating light-curve features that mimic planetary or cometary transit events. By adapting to the changing shape of the star, elliptical apertures correct for this effect, avoiding potential false positives. ADS

Atmospheric Retrieval of the Grazing WASP-34b

This paper studies the orbital and atmospheric properties of WASP-34b, a Saturn-sized exoplanet with a short, grazing orbit, from Spitzer eclipse photometry. Using elliptical photometry and multiple light-curve modeling methods, we disentangled the weak signals from the noise, determined a circular orbit, and retrieved the atmosphere's temperature. WASP-34b appears to be redder than other known planets of a similar size and temperature, suggesting a potential for unique chemistry. ADS
